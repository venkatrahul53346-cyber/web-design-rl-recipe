On the Farm — Vol. III

Hours spent outside, with reason.

Eighty-six acres of pasture, orchard, river bottom and kitchen garden. Most guests arrive for dinner and a bed; the ones who stay an extra night come for these — quiet, useful days alongside the people who grow your meal.

Experience 01 — Daily

The harvest walk.

Two hours, basket in hand, with whoever happens to be working the kitchen garden that morning — usually our head gardener, Marisol Vance. You'll pull beets, snip lovage and sorrel, cut whatever flower is opening loudest, and walk it all up to the kitchen yourself.

Most of it ends up on the pass by dinner. Some of it ends up in a small jar at your door the next morning, labelled in pencil.

— led by Marisol Vance, head gardener
DurationTwo hours
Party sizeUp to six
Included with stayNo charge
Experience 02 — Fri & Sat

A morning at the wood oven.

Theo lights the oak fire at four. You don't have to be there for that part. By the time you wander down at five-thirty, the firebox is roaring and the levain — fed the night before with our own milled Edison wheat — is ready to be shaped.

You'll shape, score and slide three loaves. Two go to the dining room. One leaves with you, still warm, wrapped in linen.

— led by Theo Halloran, head baker
DurationThree hours
Party sizeUp to four
Rate$95 / person
The Year on the Farm

What's happening, and when.

We don't run all eight experiences every week. The valley dictates the calendar — when the river drops, when the morels come up, when the cows freshen. Here is the rough shape of the year.

Early Spring
March — April

Lambing in the south barn. First nettles, the year's first morels along the timber edge. Bread mornings resume.

High Summer
June — August

Tomato glut. Stone fruit. Long evenings on the river. Sauna and plunge nights begin; harvest walks at six a.m. before the heat.

Autumn
September — November

Apple pressing in the cider house. Chanterelle hunts. The cellar tastings shift to library vintages and big reds.

Quiet Winter
December — February

House closed mid-December through late February. The pantry programme runs by appointment for returning guests only.

A Saturday in late June

One good day, hour by hour.

5:30

The bread morning

Down to the bakehouse in a sweater. Coffee from the press pot. Three loaves shaped before the first guests are awake.

8:00

Breakfast on the porch

Soft eggs, your warm loaf, butter from the Jerseys, last summer's apricot jam. The whole thing takes as long as you let it.

10:30

Harvest walk with Marisol

The basket fills with podded peas, marjoram, summer squash blossoms, and one very large head of garlic for the kitchen.

1:00

Hammock, river, nap — your call

An unscheduled afternoon is the point. We can pack a lunch in a canvas tote if you want to wander further afield.

4:00

In the cellar

Five vintages of Maison Bleu Gamay, poured by Nora. Bread, hard cheese, conversation. You'll feel it at dinner; that's fine.

7:00

The tasting menu

Eleven courses. The marjoram from this morning appears in course four. Your basket photograph is in the menu binder.

10:30

The sauna, if you've still got it in you

Cedar, then river, then the walk back to your room past the orchard. Lights off by eleven, generally.

House Notes

A few practical things.

For house guests, first

Experiences are reserved for guests staying in one of the seven rooms. We open a small number of spaces to dining-only guests on Sundays — write to ask.

Children

Welcome on the harvest walk and at the animals from age six. Casting, sauna and cellar are for guests sixteen and older.

Weather

Almost everything goes ahead in light rain. We provide waxed jackets and tall boots in your size at the mudroom by the kitchen door.

Booking

Add experiences when you reserve, or mention them in the notes — your innkeeper, Will, will confirm by email a few days before arrival.

To begin

Stay two nights. Choose one.

The room rate includes the harvest walk and access to the pasture afternoons. The rest are added when you book — or once you're here and the morning suggests itself.
