Our Story — Est. 2019

A forty-seat dining room and seven guest rooms, set among 184 acres of dryland wheat, pasture, and orchard at the mouth of the Lostine Canyon.

A restaurant that grows
most of what it serves.

Mission

To cook the valley, honestly.

Salt & Field exists to put a single, careful meal in front of forty people, four nights a week — a meal that could only have been made here, in this place, this month. We do not import a worldview from elsewhere and impose it on the Wallowas. We listen to what the field gives us. In May that means morel and asparagus and the first lamb of the year; in October it means kuri squash, mutton, dry-farmed beans, and the apples we did not press into cider.

Hospitality, to us, is the same problem as farming: pay attention, repeat the things that work, throw out the things that don't, and let the season be the season. Nothing more clever than that.

How it began

A barn, a season of borrowed plates, and a stubborn idea.

In the spring of 2018, Marit Halvorsen and Theo Reyes drove a borrowed pickup from Portland to Lostine to look at a property neither of them could afford. There was a leaning barn, a dry creek, and a farmhouse that had been empty for two winters. They bought it anyway, with help from Marit's parents and a small group of friends from the restaurant world who agreed to be repaid in dinners.

The first season — summer 2019 — was twenty-two people on a long table under string lights, plates borrowed from the diner in Enterprise, and a menu written each morning on a chalkboard nailed to the barn door. Service ran past midnight. Someone played a fiddle. By August we had a waitlist of strangers driving from Boise and Seattle, which is the moment we understood this was no longer a project but a restaurant.

Six years on, the barn is the dining room. The chalkboard is still there, but the menu is printed now, and the plates are our own. The fiddle is, occasionally, still there too.

What we believe

Five rules
of the house.

We've written these down because we have to teach them to new cooks, new servers, and new farmhands every spring. They are not aspirational. They are how the place actually runs.

01

Cook the season, not the menu.

If the chanterelles flushed yesterday, they are on the plate tonight. If the lamb isn't ready, we serve the goose instead. The menu changes weekly, sometimes nightly, and we do not apologize for it.

02

Grow it, raise it, or know who did.

Roughly seventy percent of what we serve comes from our own beds, orchard, and pasture. The rest comes from a list of fourteen producers within a two-hour drive — fishermen, foragers, dairies, and a single grain mill in La Grande.

03

Forty seats, and not one more.

We will not expand the dining room. The size of the room is the size of the meal we can make well. Adding tables would mean adding compromises, and that is the one thing we will not do.

04

Pay everyone properly, including the dishwasher.

Service is included. There is no tipping. Our lowest-paid hourly position earns $28 an hour and full health insurance. This is the entire reason the menu is priced the way it is.

05

Leave the land better than the previous tenant.

The pasture is in its fourth year of rotational grazing. The orchard is no-spray. We compost every scrap from the kitchen and the dining room. We are not certified organic and we are not seeking the certificate.
