The House

Built in 1908, kept honest.

The farmhouse was built by Olaf and Margit Halverson in the spring of 1908 and stayed in their family for ninety-one years. When we took the keys in 2019 the roof leaked into the kitchen and a barn cat lived in the linen press. We have not tried to erase any of that — we have only tried to make it warm.

There are seven rooms upstairs and a small reading room down the hall with a wood stove and most of the books we love. Floors are original Douglas fir. Walls are lime-washed in a color we mixed ourselves from the river clay. Beds are by Hästens; sheets are by Matteo; the wool throws come off our own ewes, spun in Pendleton.

No televisions. No mini-bars. No phones at the bedside — though there is one, an honest rotary one, in the hall if you need it. Breakfast is brought to your door at the hour you ask for it.

Every Stay Includes

Quiet things,
done carefully.

Breakfast at your door

Yeasted bread from our oven, butter we churn ourselves, eggs from the Wyandottes, jam from whichever fruit is having its week. Brought up to you at the hour you ask, in a wooden tray with a linen cloth.

The hour before dinner

Sherry, olives, and warm almonds in the sitting room from five until six on dinner nights — for resident guests only, before the dining room opens.

A walk in the morning

A guided walk of the farm with one of the growers at eight on Friday and Saturday mornings. Wellingtons in your size live by the back door.

Library & provisions

Twenty-four-hour access to the pantry — tea, a wheel of cheese, last night's bread, a decanter of pear brandy. Take what you like; the honor jar is on the shelf.

The bath

Hand-poured soap and bath salts from Maak in Portland, hung-to-dry linen robes, and a stack of striped towels heavy as a young dog.

The drive in

If you'd rather not navigate the gravel after dark, we'll meet you at the Joseph airstrip or the Enterprise bus station. Just let us know your arrival.
