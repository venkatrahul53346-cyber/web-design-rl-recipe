About · Mission & Mandate

The custodian of the nation's permanent public record.

The National Archive identifies, preserves, and makes available the records of government and the documentary heritage of the nation. We serve researchers, journalists, public servants, and citizens — without fee, in any of the three official languages, and to the standard required by statute.

§ 01

Our mission

Statutory · Public
Mandate

We exist to ensure that the decisions of public bodies remain legible to the people they serve. Every record we accession — a cabinet minute, a parish register, a satellite-derived flood map — is held in trust for the public and made accessible under the principle that government information belongs, by default, to those it was created to serve.

Our duty extends beyond preservation. We catalogue, conserve, digitise, translate, and explain. We train departmental records officers, advise on transfer schedules, and operate the national finding-aids infrastructure used by twenty-two ministries and more than 1,400 local authorities. Where access is restricted by law — for personal privacy, national security, or commercial sensitivity — we operate a transparent appeals process under the Freedom of Information Act.

"A nation that cannot examine its own record cannot reform it. The Archive is therefore not a warehouse of the past, but an instrument of the present." — Reading Room Inaugural Address, 1934
§ 02

Founding & history

Est. 1934 · 91 years
Origin

The Archive was established by the National Records Act of 1934, following two decades of campaigning by historians, lawyers, and parliamentary clerks who had watched the wartime correspondence of the previous government dispersed across private collections, sold at auction, or — in several documented cases — burned. The founding Act consolidated record-keeping duties that had been scattered across the State Paper Office, the Treasury Muniments, and a dozen departmental cellars, and placed them under a single Keeper accountable to Parliament.

The first reading room opened on the ground floor of the old Patent Office on Verney Street in March 1935, with a staff of nine and shelving for roughly 40,000 files. Today the Archive operates from a purpose-built facility completed in 1978, with a satellite conservation laboratory in the Northern Region and a fully digital ingestion pipeline accepting electronic records from forty-six government bodies.

1934

National Records Act

Parliament establishes the Archive as an independent statutory body and appoints the first Keeper of the Records.

1968

Public access reform

The thirty-year rule is reduced to twenty for most government series; the first public catalogue is published.

2003

Digital mandate

Electronic records are recognised as equivalent to paper; the Archive begins continuous online ingestion.

2021

Open data programme

Launch of the public dataset portal under the Open Government Licence v3.0, now hosting 4,180 datasets.

§ 03

Values that govern our work

Code of practice
01 /

Accuracy of record

Records are preserved in the form in which they were created. We do not edit, summarise, or improve them. Where transcription or translation is provided, both versions are retained and clearly distinguished.

02 /

Openness by default

Information held by public bodies is presumed to be public. Restrictions are applied only where required by statute, and every restriction is documented, time-bound, and appealable.

03 /

Equal access

No fee is charged for use of the catalogue, reading rooms, or basic reproductions. Services are provided in three official languages, with translation support on request.

04 /

Plain language

Finding aids, guidance, and decision notices are written for citizens, not specialists. We test our public-facing materials with non-expert readers before publication.

05 /

Permanence

We accession with a horizon measured in centuries. Conservation, format migration, and storage decisions are made against that standard, not against operational convenience.

06 /

Independence

The Archive does not act on the instruction of any minister in matters of accession, description, or disclosure. The Keeper reports to Parliament directly under the Act.
