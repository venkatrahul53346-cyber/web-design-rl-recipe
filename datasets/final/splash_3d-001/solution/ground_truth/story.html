Field Notes · Vol. 01

A second pair of eyes, built for the people who already read every diff.

FiledMarch 2025
BySam Okafor, co-founder
Reading time9 minutes
LocationBrooklyn, NY
01Origin

It started with a Friday afternoon revert.

In November 2023, a payment processor we were running quietly began rounding micro-transactions down instead of up. The pull request that introduced it had two approvals, eighty-seven green checks, and a one-line description. Nobody on the team was lazy. Everybody was tired.

By the time we caught it, we had charged a few thousand customers correctly and a few hundred wrong. The fix took eleven minutes. The post-mortem took eleven days. The trust took longer.

We didn't need more process. We needed someone — or something — who had also read the file, who remembered what it looked like last quarter, and who wasn't tired at 4:47 PM on a Friday.— internal Slack, two days after the incident

Pliny was the answer to that 4:47 PM problem. Not a linter. Not a policy engine. A reviewer that opens the diff with you, knows the codebase the way a long-tenured engineer knows it, and is allowed to be opinionated.

02Hypothesis

The bottleneck was never typing. It was attention.

The first generation of AI coding tools optimized for speed of generation. Auto-complete, chat-with-your-repo, agentic scaffolding. They all assume the rare resource is keystrokes. In a mature codebase, the rare resource is noticing — catching that a new helper duplicates one that already lives three folders away, or that this refactor changes the order of operations inside a transaction.

Senior engineers spend most of their day noticing. Tech leads spend most of their week noticing on behalf of others. We wanted a tool that earned a place in that loop without asking anyone to change their workflow.

03Method

Trained on your codebase, not just a codebase.

Generic models hallucinate APIs because they have read a million projects and yours is not one of them. Pliny is fine-tuned per workspace. It indexes your repository's call graph, your test fixtures, the actual return shapes of your internal libraries, the conventions inside your CODEOWNERS, and the last eighteen months of accepted review comments.

When Pliny says "this should use db.tx() instead of an ad-hoc connection," it is because seventeen other PRs in your history made the same correction. It is citing your team, not the internet.

The first comment Pliny left on our infra repo flagged a context-manager misuse in a file none of us had touched in two years. It was correct. It was also embarrassing.— Engineering lead, Series C fintech
04Restraint

We removed the things every other tool kept.

No dashboard. No "AI score." No weekly summary email pretending to be insight. No suggestions on whitespace. No suggestions on naming unless a name actually shadows an existing one. No autoplay in the demo video. No streak.

Pliny lives inside the PR. It either has something useful to say or it says nothing. The highest-performing day in our metrics is the day Pliny stayed silent on twelve consecutive PRs because they were already clean.

If a tool can't be trusted to stay quiet, it cannot be trusted to speak.

05People

Built by four engineers who got tired of being the reviewer of last resort.

The founding team came out of Stripe, Figma, Vercel, and a small flight-software shop in Cambridge. Between us we have shipped payment ledgers, real-time collaborative editors, edge runtimes, and avionics that have to be right the first time because there is no second time.

We are not a chatbot company. We are not pivoting from a chatbot company. Pliny is the only thing we are building, and the only thing we plan to build.

How we got here.

06Forward

The reviewer is a beginning, not the product.

Pull request review is where we earn the right to be in the loop. Once a team trusts Pliny to read every diff, the same context unlocks pre-merge release notes, incident retrospectives that actually cite the line that caused the outage, and architectural drift reports that read like a senior engineer wrote them — because, in a sense, one did.

We will ship those carefully, one at a time, and only after they pass the same bar: useful enough that a tech lead would protect it, quiet enough that nobody else notices it is there.

The next time someone reads your diff, let it be Pliny first.
