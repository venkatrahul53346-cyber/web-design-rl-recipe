§ 01 — PRODUCT

What it is, and what it isn't.

001 Is Pliny just another wrapper around GPT-4? +

No. Pliny runs a stack of three models — a fast retrieval pass over your repository's symbol graph, a mid-size reasoning model fine-tuned on 1.2M reviewed PRs, and a verifier model that flags hallucinations before a comment is posted. We use frontier models (Claude 3.5 Sonnet, GPT-4o) only for the reasoning step, and only when the retrieval pass returns insufficient context.

The interesting work is in the retrieval and verification layers. The chat-completion box is the boring part.

002 How is this different from CodeRabbit, Greptile, or Graphite Reviewer? +

Honest answer: there's overlap. The differences are positioning more than capability. Pliny is opinionated about quiet review — we post fewer comments, but each one is meant to be actionable. Internally we track a metric called resolve-rate: the percentage of comments that result in a code change rather than being dismissed. Pliny's resolve-rate is 71%; the industry median is 23%.

We also do not generate summaries, “PR walkthroughs,” or rubber-stamp approvals. Those are noise.

003 Does Pliny replace human code review? +

No, and we'd push back hard on any tool that claims it does. Pliny catches the boring 60% — null derefs, off-by-ones, regressions against your own prior commits, missing error handling, schema drift. The remaining 40% — architectural taste, product judgment, the question of whether this should be built at all — stays with your engineers.

Most teams report that human reviewers move faster because they're no longer hunting for typos in a 900-line diff.

004 What languages does it actually understand well? +

First-tier (deep semantic analysis, type-aware): TypeScript, Python, Go, Rust, Java, Kotlin, Swift, C#.

Second-tier (syntax-aware, lighter inference): C, C++, Ruby, PHP, Elixir, Scala, Zig.

We don't pretend to be useful on COBOL or proprietary DSLs. If you're on something obscure, mail us — we'll tell you honestly.

§ 02 — SECURITY & CODE

Where your source goes.

005 Do you train on our code? +

Never on customer code. Full stop. Our base models are trained on permissively-licensed public repositories and a curated corpus of synthetic reviews we generate in-house. Your repository is used at inference time only — we build a per-tenant retrieval index, query it, and discard ephemeral context after the review completes.

This is enforced contractually, in our DPA, and technically — model training and inference run in separate VPCs that cannot reach each other.

006 Can we self-host? +

Yes, on the Enterprise tier. We ship a Helm chart that runs on your Kubernetes cluster — typically EKS, GKE, or on-prem. You bring your own model endpoints (Bedrock, Azure OpenAI, or self-hosted Llama 3.3) and Pliny becomes a thin orchestration layer over them.

This is the right choice for regulated industries. It's also slower to set up — budget 1–2 weeks with our solutions engineer.

007 Are you SOC 2 compliant? +

SOC 2 Type II as of November 2024, audited by Prescient Assurance. ISO 27001 in flight, expected Q3 2025. GDPR DPA available on request. We are not currently FedRAMP-authorized; if that's a blocker we'll tell you up front rather than waste your time.

008 What permissions does the GitHub app require? +

Read access to repository contents and pull requests. Write access to issues and pull-request comments (so we can post reviews). That's it. We do not request write access to code, branches, or repository settings — Pliny cannot push commits, force-merge, or change protected branch rules.

§ 03 — INTEGRATION

Getting it into your flow.

009 How long does setup take? +

For GitHub Cloud teams: roughly seven minutes. Install the app, point it at your repos, wait for the initial index to build (1–4 minutes per 100k LOC), and the next PR you open gets reviewed.

GitLab self-managed, Bitbucket Server, and Gerrit take longer — usually a half-day with us on a call. Azure DevOps support is in private beta.

010 Can we tune what Pliny comments on? +

Yes. A .pliny/config.yml at the root of your repo controls severity thresholds, scoped rules per directory, and a freeform style.md that gets injected into every review prompt. Most teams write something like “we prefer early returns over nested else; flag the latter” and Pliny respects it within one or two PRs.

You can also dismiss a comment with 👎 and the model learns to suppress similar comments going forward — per repo, never globally.

§ 04 — PRICING & ACCESS

What it costs.

011 How is pricing structured? +

Per active reviewer per month. An “active reviewer” is anyone whose PR was reviewed by Pliny that month. We do not charge per repository, per LOC, or per token — those metrics correlate poorly with the value you get.

  • Team — $28 / reviewer / month. Up to 50 seats.
  • Scale — $22 / reviewer / month. Volume tiers above 50.
  • Enterprise — custom. Self-host, SSO, DPA, dedicated SE.

Open-source projects are free. We mean it; no asterisks.

012 What does “Reserve” actually get me? +

Pliny is in invite-only beta until June 2025. Reserving puts you in the queue and locks in founding-team pricing ($18/reviewer/month) for the first 12 months after general availability. We let teams in on roughly a weekly cadence, prioritized by repo size and language match against our current strengths.

There is no charge to reserve and no obligation. Pull out any time.
