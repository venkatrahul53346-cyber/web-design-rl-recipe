§ 01 — What we're doing here

The mission, plainly.

Most of the internet is a slideshow with extra steps. We exist to make the other kind — sites that breathe, sites that punch, sites where every transition earned its keep.

Locomotive partners with brands that have a story worth animating: fashion houses with attitude, architects who think in sequences, record labels, fragrance ateliers, the occasional very-confident SaaS company. We don't take on work we can't put motion into honestly. If your project needs a templated marketing site by Friday, we're flattered, but you want a different shop.

What you get when you hire us: a small senior team — no juniors hidden behind a producer — building a site end-to-end. Strategy, art direction, motion design, engineering, deployment. Twelve weeks on average. Long Slack threads. Strong opinions, kindly delivered.

§ 02 — How we got here

It started in a sublet on Rue Saint-Paul, January 2018.

Camille had just left a Paris agency that priced motion as an upcharge. Théo was freelancing between Vancouver and Montréal, exhausted by clients who wanted "a little parallax" without understanding what that meant. They met at a FITC after-party, argued about easing curves for two hours, and started Locomotive the following Monday with one client, a leased iMac, and a kettle.

Seven years later the kettle is still here. The roster is twelve. We've moved studios twice — both times within four blocks of the original sublet, because we are, evidently, a little sentimental. The work that put us on the map (Maison Verrière, the Tactile Records launch, the Banff Centre rebuild) all carry the same DNA: motion as argument, not as garnish.

We've stayed small on purpose. Growth past fifteen would mean producers, which would mean meetings about meetings, which would mean the work suffers. So we cap the roster, raise rates instead, and turn down four projects for every one we take.

Founders, on the record:

“If it doesn't move, why is it on a screen?”

— Camille Ferron & Théo Lacroix, 2018

§ 04 — How we work

Six rules,
posted on the wall.

We argue about them constantly. That's the point. A studio with no principles is a vendor.

01.

Motion is meaning, not garnish.

If an animation doesn't carry information — a hierarchy, a transition of state, a piece of personality — we delete it. Decorative motion is a tax the user pays on every scroll.

02.

Performance is a design constraint.

A 100kb hero that loads in 400ms is more beautiful than a 4mb one that doesn't. We design with the budget visible in the corner of the Figma.

03.

One opinion, in the room, on time.

The senior who made it is the one who presents it. No layers between idea and feedback. If you don't hear from the maker, you don't hear from us.

04.

Accessibility isn't a phase three ticket.

Reduced-motion, keyboard focus, screen-reader semantics — built in at sketch, not retrofitted at QA. Every animation has a still version that still tells the story.

05.

We name what we ship.

Components, easings, even color tokens get real names. "primaryButton" is not a name. "button/cobalt/loud" is a name. Naming is design.

06.

Stay small. Stay sharp.

Twelve people. Four projects at a time, maximum. We turn down good work to make great work. The math is annoying. The output is not.
