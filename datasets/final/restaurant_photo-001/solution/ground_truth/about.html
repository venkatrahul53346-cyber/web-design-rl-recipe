About the house

A restaurant that begins
at the seed packet.

Salt & Field exists to make the case that a meal can be a place — that the soil, the cook, the table, and the guest are not separate things, but four hours of one continuous argument.

The founding

We bought twelve acres of a failing apple ranch in the spring of 2019.

Maren had been cooking on the line at Atelier Crenn; Tomás had spent six seasons running the kitchen garden at Blue Hill at Stone Barns. They wanted to stop ferrying produce in coolers and start cooking ten feet from where it grew. Anderson Valley — cool nights, long days, an obstinate marine fog — turned out to be the place that would let them.

The first year was mostly fencing. The deer were prolific, the gophers were mythic, and the well needed re-drilling at depths the previous owners had stopped admitting to. We planted forty varieties of brassica, lost most of them, and learned which six wanted to live here. The restaurant opened in a converted hop kiln in October 2020, to fourteen people, on folding chairs, with one wood-fired oven and a borrowed sound system.

Four years on, the kiln seats forty. The garden is two acres of intensive market beds and another eight in perennials, grain, and pasture. We still grow most of what we serve, and we still fence against the deer every spring.

“We don’t cook from the farm. We cook because of it — on its schedule, in its mood, with whatever it has decided to give us this week.” — Maren Holloway, chef & co-owner

How we work

Five things we’ve decided to be stubborn about.

Not rules, exactly — more like the arguments we’ve already had with ourselves.

  1. 01

    Grow most of it ourselves.

    Roughly seventy percent of what reaches the plate comes from our two acres of beds or our pasture. The rest is sourced inside a hundred-mile arc — fish from Fort Bragg, lamb from Boonville, olive oil from McEvoy.

  2. 02

    Let the field write the menu.

    We don’t plan dishes more than a week ahead. If the favas are early, the favas lead. If a storm flattens the brassicas, we eat root cellar for a fortnight and don’t apologize.

  3. 03

    Pay everyone a livable wage.

    No tipping. A flat 20% service charge is built into the menu price and distributed across the entire staff — farm, kitchen, dining room. Health insurance for full-time team members from day one.

  4. 04

    Compost everything that can be composted.

    Scraps go back to the beds within forty-eight hours. We’ve sent under two cubic yards of restaurant waste to the landfill in the last calendar year. The pigs handle the rest.

  5. 05

    Keep the room to forty seats.

    We could double the dining room. We won’t. Forty is the number at which the kitchen still recognizes every plate that leaves the pass, and at which the farm can keep up without buying its way out.
