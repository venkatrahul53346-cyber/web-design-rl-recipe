The Loaf That Waits

A defence of cold fermentation, four-day schedules, and the unhurried gluten — written from a kitchen in late October, with a fridge full of dough.

Imogen Halloran·Photographs by Maartje De Waele·9 min read

A 78% hydration country loaf, retarded forty-eight hours at 4°C. The blister pattern on the crust is the first honest report you get back from the dough.

The first thing my grandmother told me about bread, when I was nine and impatient at her elbow, was that the dough did not belong to me. It belonged to itself. My job, she said, was to keep out of its way — to be the room in which it lived — and only at the very end, when it had agreed to be bread, was I allowed to call it mine. I thought this was an evasion at the time, a way of explaining why the loaf would not be ready in time for lunch.

Thirty years later, on the kitchen counter of a rented flat in Ghent, I am beginning to suspect she was telling me something operational.

Cold fermentation is the part of the bread-making conversation that most home bakers learn last, and reluctantly, because it does not photograph well and it does not happen on a Saturday morning. It is bread for people who own a calendar. The premise is plain enough: after the dough has been mixed and bulk-fermented at room temperature until it is alive but not yet enthusiastic, you put it in the refrigerator and you leave it there. Not for an hour. For a day. For two. Sometimes for four.

What happens inside that bowl, under cling film, in the dark, is roughly this. The yeast slows to a near-stop. The lactobacilli in your starter, which are happier in the cold than the yeast is, continue to work — producing the acetic and lactic acids that give a properly aged loaf its tang and its keeping quality. The proteases nibble at the gluten network, softening it without breaking it. Starches retrograde and begin to break down into the sugars that will eventually colour the crust. Nothing dramatic happens. Everything happens.

on what the cold actually does

The argument for cold fermentation is usually made on the grounds of flavour, and the flavour case is real. A loaf retarded for forty-eight hours tastes more like itself than a loaf rushed through in six. There is a winey, almost cheese-rind note in the crumb. The crust caramelises more deeply, because the cold has given the enzymes time to liberate sugars from the starch. You can taste, faintly, the wheat — which is the whole point of the exercise and is missing from most bread you will ever eat.

But the better argument, I think, is one of fit. A four-day bread fits a life in a way that a one-day bread does not. You mix on Wednesday evening after dinner; you shape on Friday morning before work; you bake on Saturday. The dough is a small, slow appointment you keep with yourself across the week. It asks for five minutes here, ten minutes there. It does not demand a whole Saturday vigil with three rises and a feeling of mild hostage.

Cold fermentation is bread for people who own a calendar. It is also, I have come to think, bread for people who are tired of being heroic in their kitchens. — Imogen Halloran

This last point is the one I keep returning to. So much of the home-baking literature, especially the sourdough literature that arrived in our kitchens with such force in 2020, treats bread as a feat. There are timers and graphs and Instagram reels of windowpane tests at 11pm. There is the implication that if your loaf is not exquisite, you simply did not try hard enough. The cold-fermented loaf has the opposite character. It rewards letting go. It punishes fussing. If you open the fridge every six hours to look at it, the only thing that happens is the dough gets slightly colder; if you ignore it for a day and a half, it does its quiet work and you arrive on baking morning to a bowl of dough that smells of orchard and yoghurt.

a workable schedule

The schedule I have settled on, after some years of trial, is unromantic. I mix a 1kg dough in the evening with cool water (around 22°C, never warm), incorporating 20% mature starter and an autolyse of forty-five minutes before adding salt. I do three sets of stretch-and-folds at thirty-minute intervals, then leave the dough at room temperature for roughly four hours, until it has gained perhaps 40% in volume — not doubled, never doubled. Into the fridge it goes, in a bowl lightly oiled, where it will live for between thirty-six and sixty hours depending on what I am doing.

Shaping happens cold, directly from the fridge. This is the part many bakers resist, because cold dough is less obliging on the bench. But it holds its structure beautifully, and the second proof — in a floured banneton, back in the fridge — can be as short as an hour or as long as overnight. I bake at 250°C in a covered Dutch oven for the first twenty minutes, then uncovered at 220°C until the crust is the colour of strong tea. The loaf will sing on the cooling rack, which is what bread does when its crust is contracting against a properly set crumb, and which is, I think, the most pleasing sound in any kitchen.

What you get back, after all this not-doing, is a loaf with an open but not cavernous crumb, a crust that shatters cleanly under the knife, and a flavour that has the depth of something that has spent some time considering itself. It keeps, wrapped in linen, for the better part of a week. It toasts on the fifth day better than most loaves toast on the first.

My grandmother, who never owned a refrigerator big enough to retard a country loaf and who would have found the whole proposition absurd, was right about the essential thing. The dough does not belong to you. The cold simply makes this more obvious. You are not making the bread — you are agreeing, for four days, to share a kitchen with it.
