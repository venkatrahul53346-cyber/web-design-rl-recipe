Field Notes · Cetara, Campania

The Anchovy Question

A small Tyrrhenian harbour still salts its catch in chestnut barrels. The fish are smaller every year. Nobody on the quay is pretending otherwise.

By Marta Lenoir·Photographs by Pieter Vandevelde
a fifteen-minute read

The boats come in at half past four, and by five the women are already cutting heads. There are eleven of them now, working at two long zinc tables that drain toward the harbour. Twenty years ago, Gaetana Marrazzo tells me, there were thirty-two. She says this without sentiment, the way a baker might mention an oven that runs cool.

Cetara sits on the southern flank of the Amalfi peninsula, a fishing village pinned between a cliff and a small grey beach. It is famous, in the narrow way Italian towns are famous, for two products that come from the same fish: salt-packed anchovies, and colatura di alici, the amber liquid that drips from them as they cure. Both are made from Engraulis encrasicolus, the European anchovy, caught at night with a lampara lamp between April and the end of July.

I have come to ask a question that the people here are tired of answering, which is whether there will be a fishery in ten years. The answer is not a number. It is a shrug, a glance at the sea, and a remark about the weight of last Tuesday's haul.

on the salting

The method is more or less the method of the second century. Anchovies are beheaded and gutted by hand — a single thumb-pull that takes practiced women under a second — then arranged in concentric layers inside a terzigno, a squat barrel of chestnut wood. Each layer is covered with coarse Sicilian sea salt. The barrel is weighted with a stone disc and left in a cellar that smells, accurately, of cellar.

After three months a clear brown liquid begins to weep from a small hole drilled in the base. This is the colatura. It is collected, exposed to sunlight in glass demijohns for several weeks, filtered through linen, and bottled. A litre sells for around forty euros at the dock and twice that in Milan. The anchovies themselves — firmer, drier, faintly meaty — are pulled out at six to nine months and packed in oil.

“The salt does not change. The barrel does not change. The fish change — smaller, leaner, and the season slips two weeks later every decade. We adjust. We have always adjusted.” Gaetana Marrazzo, salter, Cetara

Marrazzo is sixty-one. She started at the table when she was fourteen, working alongside her aunt during summer holidays from school, and she has, by her own estimate, beheaded somewhere in the region of nine million anchovies. She wears a navy apron over a navy sweater and does not look up from the table while we talk.

on the question itself

The Mediterranean anchovy biomass has, depending on whose study you read, declined between eighteen and thirty-four percent since 2005. The fish are spawning at smaller sizes. Sea-surface temperatures in the Tyrrhenian have risen 1.3°C in the same period. The Cetara fleet — eight wooden boats, none of them new — lands roughly a third of what it landed in 1995. None of this is contested. What is contested is what to do.

The town's cooperative, the Consorzio Pesca Cetara, voluntarily shortened its season by two weeks in 2019 and again in 2023. There is talk of a third reduction. The salters do not love this idea — their year compresses into a shorter, more frantic window — but they have not opposed it. “We are not stupid,” one of the younger women said, slicing.

on what to do with them

For the cook, the practical news is this: the small Cetara salt-packed anchovies remain the finest cooking anchovy available, and a teaspoon of colatura stirred into hot pasta with garlic, parsley, and good olive oil is one of the simplest serious things you can put on a plate. The recipe, called spaghetti con la colatura, contains no other ingredient. It does not need one.

Buy a bottle. Use it before it turns the colour of weak tea. When you finish it, buy another. This is, in its small way, the answer to the question.
