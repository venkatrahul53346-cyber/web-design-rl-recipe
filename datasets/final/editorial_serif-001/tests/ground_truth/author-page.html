Eline Vandermeer

writes from a narrow kitchen in Ghent, Belgium

A cook who left restaurants to write about them — and to spend long mornings learning what a fish has to teach.

Pieces14 SinceIssue 02 BeatTechnique
— the long version

Eline cooked the line at Hertog Jan and, before that, at a small fish counter in Oostende run by her aunt, which is where she learned to clean a sole in under a minute and to argue, productively, with people twice her age. She left professional kitchens in 2018 to write, and has been writing for Sandfork since the second issue — mostly about technique, occasionally about the people who refuse to let it die.

She is the kind of writer who will telephone a cheesemaker in the Vosges to confirm a single sentence, then rewrite the paragraph anyway. Her pieces tend to start with a small, almost domestic observation — the way a knob of butter behaves when it meets a hot pan, the silence after a stockpot is finally turned off — and widen from there into something larger about the way we eat now.

“Technique is just attention with its sleeves rolled up. Most of what people call talent in a kitchen is somebody, somewhere, having been patient with you.”

— from Issue 05, “The Long Braise”

Outside of Sandfork, she has written for De Standaard Magazine, Vittles, and the now-defunct paper journal Boerderij. Her first book — a slow, opinionated field guide to braising on the North Sea coast — will be published by Hannibal in spring 2026. She teaches a once-yearly weekend class on stocks and mother sauces from a converted slaughterhouse in Sint-Niklaas; the next one is already full, but the waiting list is open.

If you have a story for her — an obsessive home cook in your village, a baker who refuses to retire, a pantry ingredient that deserves a proper essay — she would like to hear about it. She answers letters slowly but answers them.
