About the magazine

A journal for the
kitchen-minded.

Sandfork is a printed cooking journal published twice a year in Antwerp. We make a magazine for people who cook on Tuesdays, not just on holidays — readers who want to know why a sauce breaks, who keeps the last good anchovy fishery alive, and what a 78-year-old baker in Trabzon does with the heel of yesterday's loaf.

Our mission

To take cooking seriously without taking ourselves seriously — and to put the result on paper, where it belongs.

We believe the best food writing isn't reverent and it isn't ironic. It is curious. It treats a chickpea and a turbot with the same patience. It asks the cook what they were thinking on the third try, not the first. Every issue of Sandfork is built around that posture: long-form reporting, tested recipes with their bruises showing, and photography that respects the food enough to leave it alone.

We are not interested in trend coverage, restaurant tier-lists, or the personality economy of the kitchen. We are interested in heat, salt, time, and the people who organise their lives around them.

How we started

A newsletter that outgrew its inbox.

Sandfork began in the spring of 2021 as a Tuesday-morning email between four friends: a pastry cook, a food anthropologist, a designer, and a print buyer who happened to own a Heidelberg press in Ghent. The original list had eighty-three people on it. The recipes were tested twice and the essays ran long, and within a year we noticed something inconvenient — we were sending out documents that wanted to be read on paper.

The first printed issue went out in October 2022, mailed flat in a stiff envelope to nine hundred subscribers across Belgium, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom. We chose a serif typeface designed for books, a paper weight stout enough to live on a counter, and a format wide enough for a recipe to breathe across two pages. We never used the word “launch.”

Three years on, Sandfork mails to forty-one countries. The newsletter still goes out on Tuesdays. We still test every recipe at least twice, and we still argue about the bread chapter.

What we hold to.

Five working principles — not a manifesto, just the things we keep coming back to when an issue is being argued into shape.

  1. 01

    Test it twice, then once more in someone else's kitchen.

    No recipe is published in Sandfork until it has worked in at least two homes that aren't ours. We pay our testers. We list them. If a dish only works with a particular pan, oven, or vendor, we say so on the page.

  2. 02

    The cook is the story, not the magazine.

    We profile working cooks — in restaurants, in homes, in the back of bakeries — and we ask them about labour, training, money, and family before we ask them about plating. We send them their quotes before we run them.

  3. 03

    Paper, when paper is the right answer.

    Sandfork is printed because recipes are read in kitchens, with floury hands, next to a hot pan. A magazine that survives that is worth making. The web version of any piece goes up four weeks after the issue mails — never before.

  4. 04

    No undisclosed gifts, no sponsored recipes, ever.

    We accept no review copies of cookware we keep, no comped meals we then write about, and no payment for editorial mention of a product. Subscribers fund the magazine. That's the entire commercial arrangement.

  5. 05

    If we got it wrong, the correction goes on the page it lived on.

    Recipes get reprinted with their fixes. Essays get amended with a dated note. We don't quietly edit and pretend the first version didn't happen — that's how cooking gets learned, after all.

The masthead.

Six people make Sandfork between Antwerp, Ghent, and Lisbon. A wider circle of testers, photographers, and translators — credited in each issue — make it possible.

Editor in chief
Mira Hellebaut

Former pastry cook at In de Wulf and Hertog Jan. Edits the recipe chapter and reads every essay twice before it goes to typesetting.

Features editor
Theo Asante

Trained as a food anthropologist at SOAS. Commissions the long reads and the contributor profiles; spends most of January in Ghana with a notebook.

Art director
Inge Vermeulen

Designs the magazine and the website. Built the original masthead in a borrowed studio in Borgerhout. Keeps the colour palette honest.

Production & print
Joost Verlinden

Owns the press in Ghent. Forty years in commercial printing; the only one of us who can read a colour proof without squinting.

Recipe editor
Lília Marques

Cooks out of Lisbon. Coordinates the testing pool and writes the “Pantry” column — the one about a single ingredient at a time.

Photo editor
Sara Nakamura

Photographer turned editor. Believes food should be lit like a portrait, not a product, and argues for it issue after issue.

Copy chief
Hannes Lievens

Reads everything last. Holds the line on em-dashes, weights versus volumes, and the proper plural of “focaccia.”

Subscriber & circulation
Yusra Akin

Runs the mail-out, the stockist relationships, and the very small office cat. The reason your issue arrives flat and on time.
