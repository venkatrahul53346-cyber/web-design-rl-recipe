Chapter 00 · The origin of Pliny

We built Pliny because good code review was the first thing teams quietly stopped doing.

This is the long version. How a tool that sits inside your pull requests came to exist, what we learned shipping it to seventy engineering teams, and why we think the next decade of software belongs to the reviewers.

Written by Anya Petrov, co-founder March 14, 2025 Brooklyn, NY 18 min read
01 ⁄ 06 The empty inbox

By 2023, nobody on our team was reviewing pull requests anymore.

Not really. We approved them. We left the polite emoji. Occasionally, on a Tuesday, someone with a free afternoon would leave a comment about variable naming and we'd all pretend that was the point of code review.

The actual point — catching the dumb regression, the off-by-one, the auth check you forgot, the migration that locks the table for four minutes in production — was happening less and less. Not because we were lazy. Because we were shipping forty PRs a day and a senior engineer's attention is a finite, expensive thing.

The numbers were ugly when we finally looked. Mean time-to-review at our previous company had crept from 47 minutes to 6 hours and 12 minutes over eighteen months. Comments-per-PR was down 71%. Approval-without-comment was up to 64%. Reviews had become a ritual we performed to keep CI green.

That was the moment Pliny started, though we didn't call it that yet. We called it the_intern.py and it lived on Marc's laptop.

02 ⁄ 06 A weekend hack

The first version was 312 lines of Python and embarrassingly useful.

Marc wired GPT-4 to a GitHub webhook over a long weekend in October. The bot read the diff, the surrounding files, and the last twenty commits to the same paths. It posted one comment per PR with the heading // the intern thinks:. Half of what it said was wrong, fifteen percent was obvious, and thirty-five percent was the kind of catch a senior engineer would have made on their best day.

Within a week, our team had started waiting on the intern before merging. Not because it was right, but because reading its take forced you to look at your own PR with fresh eyes. It was a rubber duck that could read.

Then someone outside the team asked if they could install it. Then a friend at another company. Then a stranger from the internet. By December the bot was running on forty-one repositories and we had a Stripe account and a problem: the_intern.py was, in fact, very bad software, and people were starting to depend on it.

We didn't want to build another copilot. We wanted to build the colleague who reads your PR at 11pm and quietly asks are you sure about line 84?
— Marc Helou · co-founder, CTO · ex-Stripe, ex-Linear
03 ⁄ 06 Things we got wrong

We spent six months building the wrong product, three times.

The first wrong version tried to fix the code itself. We'd post a PR-on-a-PR with the bug rewritten. Engineers hated it. It felt like a junior taking over the keyboard. Worse, the suggestions were stylistically wrong in ways that revealed Pliny didn't actually know the codebase — it knew Python, not your Python.

The second wrong version tried to rank PRs by risk, a single number 0–100. Engineers ignored the number. The number was either obviously high (everyone already knew the migration was scary) or obviously wrong.

The third wrong version tried to chat. You could @-mention the bot. Nobody @-mentioned the bot. People want a reviewer who shows up unprompted, not one they have to summon.

The version that worked — the version that became Pliny — does one thing. It posts concrete, line-anchored suggestions the moment a PR opens, and shuts up if it has nothing useful to add. The hardest engineering problem turned out to be teaching it when to stay quiet.

Six rules we wrote on the wall

PLINY ⁄ INTERNAL ⁄ 2024-02-09
RULE 01

Silence is a feature.

A reviewer who comments on every PR is noise. Pliny is allowed — encouraged — to approve without speaking. We measure the ratio of useful comments to total comments, and we throw away anything that drops it.

RULE 02

Line numbers, not paragraphs.

Every suggestion is anchored to a specific line, with a one-sentence rationale and, where possible, a diff. We do not write essays in the comments. Senior engineers don't read essays in the comments.

RULE 03

The codebase is the ground truth.

Pliny indexes your repository — every function, every test, every commit — and is fine-tuned per-customer. A suggestion that doesn't match your idioms is a suggestion we shouldn't have made.

RULE 04

Wrong is worse than late.

We will hold a comment for ninety seconds to verify it against the test suite rather than ship a confident hallucination. Bug reports about false positives are P0 internally.

RULE 05

The human still merges.

Pliny does not approve, does not block, does not gate CI. It is a reviewer with opinions and no merge rights. Authority belongs to the engineer with the cursor.

RULE 06

Earn the next review.

Every comment is a small bet on the engineer's attention. If we waste it, they will stop reading us, and then we are worse than nothing. Each review must earn the next one.

04 ⁄ 06 The seventy teams

By mid-2024, Pliny was reviewing roughly nine thousand pull requests a week.

We sat with seventy engineering teams during the closed beta. A small fintech in Berlin where every PR touches money. A games studio in Montreal where the codebase is half C++, half a custom scripting language nobody else uses. A Series C SaaS company where the median tenure is fourteen months and tribal knowledge evaporates every quarter.

The same patterns kept showing up. The catches that mattered most weren't the clever ones. They were the boring ones — the env var you forgot to add, the feature flag you didn't gate, the index you didn't create on a column you were about to query a million times a day.

One team in Stockholm shipped a payment regression that would have cost them an estimated €180,000 in failed transactions over a weekend. Pliny had flagged it on the PR, in the third comment down, with the text amount is in cents on line 41 but euros on line 67 — likely off by 100×. The reviewer had marked the comment resolved without addressing it. We learned a lot from that one. We changed how Pliny escalates when a comment is dismissed without code change.

05 ⁄ 06 What we believe

The interesting question is no longer can the model write the code. It's who reads it.

Generation is a solved problem in the sense that matters. Every team we talk to is now producing more code than they can responsibly review. The constraint has moved one step downstream: not can you make it, but do you trust what you just made.

We think the next decade of software belongs to the reviewers. The teams who ship with confidence will be the ones who have built the most rigorous, the most patient, the most institutionally-paranoid review culture. And review at the volume modern engineering organizations produce is not a problem you solve by hiring more humans. It is a problem you solve by giving every human a tireless second pair of eyes.

That's it. That's the whole thesis. Pliny is the tool that makes review possible again at the velocity of 2025 engineering. Not a replacement for the reviewer. The reviewer's caffeine.

06 ⁄ 06 What's next

We are not finished. We are barely started.

The current version of Pliny reads a pull request the way a careful senior engineer would on a good morning. The version we are building next will read the pull request the way a careful senior engineer would on a good morning with the entire history of every incident your team has ever had loaded into their head.

We're working on tighter integrations with incident tooling, so Pliny can connect a diff to the postmortem it should have learned from. We're working on team-level review patterns, so Pliny can surface the kinds of bugs your team specifically ships, not the kinds of bugs the internet at large ships. We're working on a self-hosted build that runs entirely inside your VPC, because we are tired of hearing that the most security-conscious teams cannot use the tool that would help them most.

If any of that sounds like something you'd want to try, the beta is open. We read every signup form. We write back, usually within a day, usually with too many questions.
