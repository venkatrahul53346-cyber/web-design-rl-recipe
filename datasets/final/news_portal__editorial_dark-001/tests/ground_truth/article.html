The stubborn case for braising at ninety-two degrees

Every cookbook tells you to bring it to a simmer and turn it down. Nobody tells you what that actually means. After three months and forty-one shoulders of pork, we measured.

By Hannah Aldous · Photographs by Iyo Tanaka · Published 14 November · 18 min read

The first time I tried to write down what a proper braise looks like, I gave up after a paragraph. The language was all there — a bare tremble at the surface, a single bubble every few seconds, the lid set ajar — and none of it told you anything you could repeat. Cooking writing is full of these little betrayals: descriptions that mean everything to the person who already knows, and nothing to the person who doesn't. So in late August, slightly out of frustration and mostly out of stubbornness, I bought a thermometer and started measuring.

What follows is the result of forty-one shoulders of pork, two whole oxtails, a quantity of lamb necks I'd rather not itemise, and a small notebook now permanently stained with cider. The headline finding is unromantic: a braise that has been described to you, your whole cooking life, as a gentle simmer is in fact happening at about 92°C. Not 100. Not boiling. Ninety-two, give or take a degree. And the difference between 92 and 96 is the difference between meat you want to eat and meat that has been, in the technical sense, ruined.

i.The thermometer in the lid

I should say at the start: I am not a scientist and this is not a paper. I drilled a small hole in the wooden knob of my Le Creuset, threaded a probe through it, and ran the cable out under the edge of the lid. The probe sat about an inch below the surface of the liquid — close enough to register the cooking water, far enough from the meat to read the bath and not the joint. I cooked, I logged, I ate the evidence.

The first surprise was how badly I had been reading the surface. When I turned the hob to the lowest setting on my gas range and waited until the liquid did what cookbooks call a gentle simmer — those soft, occasional plops, three or four a minute — the probe read 98°C. When I dropped the heat further, to what looked like nothing at all, the temperature held at 94. The bubbles I had been chasing for fifteen years were already too much.

The second surprise was how much better the meat got, three degrees down.

ii.Why 92, and not 96

Collagen — the connective tissue that gives shoulders, shanks and tails their structure and their reluctance — begins to break down into gelatin at around 65°C. The conversion is slow at first and accelerates with both heat and time. At 100°C you are converting collagen quickly, but you are also driving water out of the muscle fibres at a frightening rate. The meat shreds, but it shreds dry; the strands hold together only because of the sauce around them.

“At 92°C the meat is doing two things at once: surrendering its collagen, and keeping its water. Push four degrees higher and it gives you only the first.”
— Notes, 4 October

At 92°C the conversion still happens — it just takes a bit longer. The reward is that the muscle fibres are not being squeezed dry. You end up with meat that is genuinely tender in a way I had only previously achieved by accident: meat that yields to a fork, but does not collapse when you try to lift a piece intact. That distinction — yielding versus collapsing — turns out to be the whole game.

There is a related point about the sauce. A braise cooked at 92 reduces more slowly than one at 98, which means your liquid is in the pan longer and is doing more work on the aromatics. The cider in our recipe below spends an extra forty minutes pulling apart sage stems and onion skins that, at a higher heat, would have already given up.

A note on hobs Induction will hold 92°C indefinitely if you can find the right setting; the trouble is that most rings tick between two temperatures rather than holding one. Gas is more forgiving because it is less precise — you adjust by eye and ear. Electric coils are the worst. If you only have an electric hob, skip ahead to part four; the oven is your friend.

iii.The collar test

I needed a way to describe 92°C without making everyone buy a thermometer. After some experimenting — and a lot of standing over pots looking at them sideways — I settled on what I have started calling the collar test. It is this: lift the lid of your pot a centimetre or two, and look at the ring of liquid where it meets the wall of the pan.

At 98 or 100, the collar is in constant motion. There is a band of small bubbles continuously breaking against the side of the pot, like a tide line. At 92, the collar is still. The surface in the centre may have one or two slow blisters a minute, but the edge where it meets the metal looks completely flat. If the collar is still, you are braising. If the collar is moving, you are simmering, and you should turn the heat down.

iv.A short defence of the oven

Most braising advice eventually arrives at the same place: put the pot in the oven. There is a reason. An oven set to 130°C will hold the contents of a covered pot at — you guessed it — about 92°C, more or less indefinitely, with almost no attention from you. You do not need a thermometer. You do not need to watch the collar. You need an oven that does roughly what its dial says it does, which is admittedly a larger ask than it should be.

If your oven runs hot (most do), drop the dial to 120. If it runs cold, push it to 140. The interior of a covered braising pot lags well behind the air around it, which is why oven braising is so much more forgiving than hob braising: the heat is being delivered slowly, from all sides, through a buffer of steam. There is no scorching, because the bottom of the pot is no hotter than the top.

I cooked the last twelve shoulders this way and then re-cooked the recipe on the hob, to be sure I wasn't fooling myself. The oven versions were, on balance, slightly better — partly because the sauce reduced more slowly, partly because I wasn't fussing with them. The hob versions had a fractionally more interesting crust around the meat where it touched the pot, which I'll concede is a real thing worth having. Take your pick.

One last thing about the lid

Leave it on. I know — half the books you have tell you to set it ajar, to crack it, to put a wooden spoon under one edge. They are trying, slightly clumsily, to compensate for the fact that you were cooking too hot in the first place. At 92, evaporation is slow enough that you do not need to vent the pot. If your sauce is too thin at the end, lift the meat out, set it somewhere warm, and reduce the liquid hard on the hob for five minutes. You will get a glossier, more concentrated sauce in less time than three hours of cracked-lid evaporation ever managed.

The braise has been around for several hundred years longer than the thermometer. People have been making excellent versions of it for almost all of that time without measuring anything. I am not suggesting that you must measure. I am suggesting that if you have ever felt that your braises were nearly there but not quite — if the meat has been a little stringy, or a little dry, or a little tough in the wrong places — the chances are excellent that you have been cooking three degrees too hot. Drop the heat. Watch the collar. The pot will sit there, very nearly silent, and do everything else for you.

Hannah Aldous is Sandfork's technique editor. She is currently testing the same theory on bean cookery, with mixed results.
