A small glossary, for the things we keep saying

The pantry has its own vocabulary, and we'd rather you didn't have to Google in the middle of a recipe.

Colatura co·la·TOO·ra
An amber liquid drawn off barrels of salted anchovies aged in Cetara, on the Amalfi coast. Treat it like fish sauce's quieter, more expensive cousin — a few drops on pasta with garlic and chilli.
Fleur de sel flur duh SELL
The thin, flaky crust that forms on the surface of evaporating sea-water pools. Harvested by hand. For finishing only; cooking with it is a small heresy.
Bottarga bo·TAR·ga
The salted, pressed and air-dried roe of grey mullet or tuna. Grate sparingly over hot pasta, raw fennel, or buttered toast. A Sardinian luxury that lasts months in the fridge.
Saba SAH·ba
Unfermented grape must reduced to a thick, dark, slightly bitter syrup. The pre-Roman ancestor of balsamic vinegar. Excellent over yoghurt, walnuts, or roast pumpkin.
Garum GA·rum
Fermented fish sauce of the ancient Mediterranean, currently being revived by a handful of Spanish and Danish producers. Aged 12 to 36 months. Pungent, deep, savoury.
Pekmez pek·MEZZ
A Turkish reduction of fruit juice — most often grape, mulberry, or carob — to a molasses-thick syrup. Used in dressings, on bread with tahini, or stirred into yoghurt at breakfast.
Verjuice VAIR·joos
The pressed juice of unripe grapes. Sharp without being acidic in the way vinegar is. Medieval cooks lived on it; modern cooks should keep a small bottle.
Doenjang DEN·jang
A Korean fermented soybean paste — earthier and funkier than miso, with a long, slow finish. The base for jjigae and a dozen weeknight stews.
Lardo LAR·do
Back fat from pork, cured with salt, rosemary and pepper for at least six months in marble basins. Shaved thin on hot toast it melts on contact. The Tuscan version, from Colonnata, is the standard.
