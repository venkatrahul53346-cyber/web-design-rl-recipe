What this is

Notes from the cooking class, twice a year on paper.

Sandfork is a journal for people who already know how to cook and want to cook better. Long recipes that explain themselves. Technique pieces that don't lie about how hard a thing is. Profiles of the people who make food worth eating.

No recipe carousels. No fifteen-minute weeknight anything. We publish when there is something to say, and twice a year we set it in type, print it in Norwich on uncoated stock, and send it to subscribers in plain brown paper.

Live now · Lyon, 04.12 local

Thirty-six hours at Saint-Jean, a bakery that hasn't closed since 1962.

Iola is in the back room with Patrice, the third-generation baker, and a levain that lives in a wooden trough older than any of us. Updates from the oven, the till, and the queue that starts forming at six.

  • 04.12Scoring the boules. Patrice does it in two cuts, never four. The shape of the ear, he says, is a question of confidence.
  • 03.40The levain is loud tonight. A faint hiss when the cloth is lifted — a good sign, apparently.
  • 02.55First load in. 240°C falling to 220°C. Twenty-eight loaves, thirty-eight minutes.

From the editor

A note from Iola, on what we're trying to do with Nº 07 and why bread keeps pulling us back.

When we started Sandfork, we said we'd publish only the things we'd want to read on a Sunday evening with the dishes still to do. Issue Nº 07 is the closest we've come.

It is also, fair warning, the longest. Forty-eight more pages than last spring, a foldout map of a Lyon arrondissement, and a recipe index at the back that I am quietly proud of.
