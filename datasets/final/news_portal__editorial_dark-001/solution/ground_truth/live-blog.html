Thirty-six hours at Saint-Jean, the Lyon bakery that refuses to open before dawn

Marguerite Vasseur bakes 240 loaves a day from a basement oven her grandfather built in 1953. We are following her from the first mix through Sunday lunch service — flour temperatures, sleep deprivation, an unexpected delivery of chestnut flour, and the queue that has formed every morning since 1971.

06:48 Saturday — just now By Claire Bénichou

The queue starts forming in the dark, as it always does.

It is still twenty minutes before the door opens and there are already nine people on the cobbles outside, hands buried in coat sleeves, breath visible. The man at the front — Henri, retired, comes every Saturday since his wife died — has been here since five forty. He doesn't talk about the bread. He talks about the rain.

Inside, Marguerite is loading the third batch. The oven has been running since one a.m. and the bakery now smells the way only a working bakery in the small hours smells: hot stone, caramelised crust, and something faintly sweet from yesterday's brioche dough fermenting in the corner.

“If they come before the door is open, that is their business. The bread is ready when it is ready.”— Marguerite Vasseur
05:30 Saturday By Tom Ardoin

An unexpected delivery: 12 kilos of chestnut flour from the Ardèche

A van pulled up on Rue des Trois-Maries at five fifteen — earlier than expected — carrying a sack of stone-milled chestnut flour from a producer in Vals-les-Bains. Marguerite hadn't planned for it. She does now. The Sunday loaves, she tells me, will go 15% chestnut, 85% T80. “It will be sweet. People will think I have added sugar. I have not added sugar.”

The chestnut flour, milled four days ago. It smells, improbably, of toasted hazelnut and damp wood.

Recipe sidebar · added 05:42

Chestnut & honey loaf — Marguerite's working ratio

  • T80 wheat flour 850 g
  • Chestnut flour, stone-milled 150 g
  • Stiff levain (overnight) 220 g
  • Water, 26°C 720 g
  • Sea salt 20 g
  • Chestnut honey, optional 1 tbsp

Autolyse 45 min. Three folds at 30 min intervals. Bulk 4–5 hours at 23°C. Shape, retard overnight at 6°C. Bake 250°C falling to 220°C, 38 minutes, dark.

04:12 Saturday By Claire Bénichou

First batch is in. Twenty-four kilos of dough, four minutes of total silence.

There is a particular calm at four in the morning when the oven door comes down. Marguerite scored each loaf with two parallel cuts and a third diagonal — the Saint-Jean signature, unchanged since her grandfather — then loaded twelve at a time onto the peel. Nobody spoke. Étienne, her one apprentice, watched from the doorway with a clipboard.

The crust will set in seven minutes. After that there's nothing to do but wait, and Marguerite uses the time the way she always uses it: she makes coffee, sits on the upturned crate by the flour bin, and reads two pages of whatever book is on the shelf. Tonight: Colette.

02:55 Saturday By Tom Ardoin

The levain is reading 17.2°C — a degree colder than she'd like

Marguerite keeps her starter in a covered ceramic crock on a shelf above the proving oven, which radiates a gentle warmth. The target is 18.5°C at this stage of bulk fermentation. Tonight, the November cold has got into the basement faster than expected and the crock is reading 17.2°C on the probe thermometer.

Her response is unhurried: she pulls the dough tubs six inches closer to the oven, drapes them with a second linen cloth, and adjusts her schedule by twenty-three minutes. “It will catch up,” she says, without looking up. “It always catches up.”

01:18 Saturday By Claire Bénichou

Pre-shaping the country loaves, by hand, in the half-dark

Saint-Jean has no dough divider. Marguerite weighs each piece on a brass scale her father bought at a flea market in 1968 — 920 grams, every time, to within five grams. She has been doing this for thirty-one years. Her hands know it before the scale confirms it.

The dough is loose, very hydrated, and her shaping is brief: a single fold, a quarter turn, a gentle round. She works under one bulb. The basement is forty square metres. The whole operation, including the oven, lives in this room.

23:40 Friday By Tom Ardoin

She lights the wood-fire oven the same way every Friday: with yesterday's bread.

The oven is a deck oven built in 1953 by Marguerite's grandfather and a mason from Vienne, brick by brick, in this same basement. It takes four hours to reach temperature. Marguerite starts the fire with the heels of the previous week's unsold loaves, broken into kindling and stacked dry. “Nothing is wasted,” she says. “Not even me.”

The oven mouth, eleven forty p.m. Friday. Stone temperature: 142°C, climbing.

22:10 Friday By Eli Park

Mixing the levain refresh — 1:2:2, by feel and by smell

Tonight's refresh is one part mother, two parts water, two parts flour. Marguerite doesn't measure the water; she pours from a tin pitcher until the dough "sounds right" against the wooden spoon. The first time I asked her what "sounds right" meant, she laughed for a long time and then refused to answer.

The starter is fed and covered. It will triple by one a.m. We will know when it is ready because a small dot of starter, dropped into a glass of room-temperature water, will float. This is the only test she trusts.

21:30 Friday — Day 1 begins By Eli Park

We arrive at Saint-Jean. Marguerite is already three hours into the day.

The bakery has no sign on Rue des Trois-Maries. You find it by the queue, or by the smell, or because someone has told you. We came down a narrow stone staircase past two recycling bins and a bicycle, and there she was at the wooden bench, sleeves to the elbow, weighing flour by the kilogram into a galvanised tub.

She did not look up. She said, "You're early. The bread isn't." Then she handed Eli a brush and asked him to oil the proving baskets. That is how the next thirty-six hours began.
